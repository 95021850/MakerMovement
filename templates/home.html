3D Printed Ebike Solution

Designed and fabricated by Lawrence Chang

Driven by my laziness, I decided to make an ebike because I got tired of biking to school every morning. In order to make keep this electric bike system cheap and accessible, all custom parts can be 3d printed and does not require any machining. I wanted a functioning bike as fast as possible and for as little money as possible. The bikeâ€™s motion system was designed in Solidworks and most parts were sourced from ebay. The conversion is meant for any generic single speed fixie bike with a flip flop hub and was up and running for under $250.

Filament Recycler

Designed and fabricated by Lawrence Chang

I had recently purchased a 3D printer after saving up enough money from selling fidget spinners. After accumulating many failed prints, I built this recycler to remelt the plastic mulch into useable filament to help negate the costs of new filament. The mechanisms consist of an auger bit inside of a steel tube to push the plastic mulch, and a heating element, controlled by a PID temp controller module and a solid state relay. Although the filament is far from professional quality and likely has many impurities, it can still be used for rougher prints.

Hub Mid-Drive Ebike

Designed and fabricated by Lawrence Chang

I purchased a used hub motor and wanted to electrify another bike. In order to keep the center of gravity between the wheels, and to avoid having to lace the hub motor into my wheel, I decided to make it mid drive. After machining a few aluminum parts on the lathe and mill, adding a second chainring, and a crank arm free wheel, the motion system was up and running. The hub motor housing is bolted into the water bottle holder screws in the frame of the bike. The batteries are housed in harbour freight ammo cases and consist of recycled 18650 lithium ion cells. Using 3D printed battery cases, I was able to create two 13s 4p battery packs, giving me about 20 miles of range.

Desktop CNC Router

Designed by Lawrence Chang

As I am a part of FRC robotics team #8, my team could greatly benefit from a cnc router capable of machining aluminum profiles for the robots that we build. Increasing our part output with the flexibility of multiple machines is ideal for an FRC robotics environment. I wanted to keep the entire project under 1.5k using generic ebay, and aliexpress parts. The router is still in the design phase and is based off of aluminum t-slot extrusion.
